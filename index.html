Bauhaus

1919–1933

Founded in Weimar, Germany, the Bauhaus school sought to unify diverse artistic disciplines into a Gesamtkunstwerk, "total work of art." The school’s curriculum began with the preliminary course Vorkurs, where students experimented with material properties, primary colors, and geometric abstraction. The Bauhaus discarded historical ornament in favor of Modernism, supporting the principle Form Follows Function, where the aesthetic of an object was determined by its utility. The school moved to Dessau and later Berlin before its closure, leaving a legacy of functionalist design that integrated art into every aspect of modern architecture.

Gesamtkunstwerk Vorkurs geometric abstraction Modernism Form Follows Function

Examples: Bauhaus Building Dessau by Walter Gropius (UNESCO) MoMA: Bauhaus 1919–1933 Workshops for Modernity

Not to be confused with Art Deco, because Bauhaus emphasizes industrial utility and the removal of ornament, whereas Art Deco focuses on decorative luxury and extravagance.

Swiss Style

International Typographic Style · 1950s–1970s

Emerging in Switzerland and Germany, the Swiss Style—also known as the International Typographic Style—refined the principles of the Bauhaus into a rational and mathematical design language. Central to this movement was a strict grid system utilized to organize content, ensuring a consistent visual hierarchy across diverse media. Swiss Style championed visual harmony through asymmetrical balance, placing elements with intentional weight rather than mirroring them. Designers incorporated objective photography and sans-serif typefaces such as Helvetica to emphasize logic and legibility over subjective artistic expression. The movement achieved a clean, structured aesthetic, becoming the international standard for corporate and universal communication.

Not to be confused with Minimalism, because Swiss Style is a specific communication methodology based on mathematical grids, while Minimalism is a broader aesthetic of reduction.

New Wave Typography

1970s–1980s

Emerged from the Basel School of Design in Switzerland, New Wave Typography emerged as a Postmodernism reaction against the rigid constraints of the Swiss Style. While it maintained a foundation in Swiss design, it sought to break the rules of its predecessor. Designers abandoned the "less is more" philosophy by using a deconstructed grid to create intentional visual tension and convoluted compositions that challenge viewers’ perception. The movement embraced layering typography, often overlapping text and graphics to build depth. By integrating irregular letterspacing and mixed typeweights, designers moved away from neutrality toward expressive, jagged visual rhythms. This shift prioritized individual interpretation and subjectivity over the sterile, logical systems of earlier modern design.

Postmodernism deconstructed grid layering typography irregular letterspacing mixed typeweight

Examples: Kunstkredit 76/77 Poster by Wolfgang Weingart (V&A) · Does It Make Sense? by April Greiman (MoMA)

Not to be confused with Grunge Typography, because New Wave specifically subverts professional Swiss design principles through technical deconstruction, whereas Grunge is a total rejection of professional typesetting.
